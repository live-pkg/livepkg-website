Random project - Large multiplayer & custom backend#

One of the projects I’ve been toying with is a multiplayer game focused on large room sizes and social features. Not something too hard in theory, but I had some “unique” constraints and personal wants in this project which is kind of like a self-challenge.

This is probably the project I’ll talk most about on a lot of these devlogs as it’s the current thing I’m working on and feel like it’s a good fit for a public showcase.

So, about those wacky special constraints:#

Initially I knew I’d have limited options, my previous experience was with using steamworks multiplayer API and some high-level abstractions but this wasn’t going to work here. I wanted something where I had control over what packets got sent where and when to maybe limit bandwidth and processing power needed, and I couldn’t rely on steam since it wasn’t primarily a steam game (if it will ever be). Additionally, I don’t have money to host my own servers or really want to bother maintaining them and scaling them, especially due to lack of experience. Finally my lack of experience in this subject in GENERAL would mean I’d want to stray away from APIs and services I don’t know much about or architectures like authoritative server which seemed daunting and hard to build/maintain when I have no idea how to do that.

I had thought about using something akin to webRTC for a simple P2P networking system, cuts out the middle man and the need for a server which would’ve been great! Although the limitation of only P2P connections only became more and more impossible when I started thinking about multiple rooms and servers, deciding who is “host”, migration in case of disconnection or other issues, not something I was keen on. Plus this wasn’t really true, you need SOME way for your clients to know how to first begin talking to eachother and that means relying on a relay server (which is not inherently a bad idea, but also doesn’t mean it’s free) and other issues like network limitations and NAT types not allowing connections which would need some OTHER relay service and then I just kinda lost hope.

The indie web#

As it happens, iris was browsing indie web rings, as one does, and stumbled across Elle’s homepage. She sent it to me and it was a really radical site with all the fun and intrigue you’d want in a personal site with a thousand corridors and features to explore. That’s when I noticed these blurry icons floating around, but something instantly caught my eye, they weren’t moving in a way that seemed programmable, far too natural and alive, and even sometimes reacting to me? That’s when it clicked! This website was like a multiplayer session where your cursor position (along with stuff like messages and icons and colors) was synced with other people viewing the page! That was rad, and I had to know how they were doing this.

Small amount of digging later, yep there it is! Something called “partykit”, and after some more diggin it seemed to be everything I was looking for. It was cheap (basically free), easy, didn’t need to know about scaling or backends or connections, it kind of just worked! And when I saw it just used websockets (something that game engines can communicate with and have multiplayer abstractions for) I was hooked. At some point it was merged with cloudflare and it was based on cloudflare “durable objects”, not going to go into this in this blog but it’s a very cool piece of kit and I decided to take the hardest route possible, learn cloudflare workers, learn cloudflare durable objetcs, not use JS or TS (which is the language they used) and rely on a very early and poorly documented “Python workers” (I was comfortable with python) and bash my head against the wall for the greater part of a day.

Why is it called a backend and not a rearend#

Soooo…. who would’ve thought that through doing all this I had gained a tiny amount of required knowledge about backends and was basically just making my own super simple game server and packets and whatnot? I’ll spare you the details of the immense amount of pain I had to go through, either through absolutely terrible AND OFTEN WRONG documentation or trying to learn what every webstandard I had to use was (oh my god please I don’t wanna read MDN anymore). But I had done it! A few days later I had succesfully communicated with my client and the “server” to some degree! Now…. I had to work on… other clients….

Queue torture for a few days and also coming up with how everything needed to be in the future which ofc causes the whole “well I should just do it like that now so I won’t have to change everything over!” and then seeing how daunting of a task it was and just not doing anything. But nothing a bunch of spaghetti and “good enough” band-aids that will come back to haunt me later on won’t fix!!!! To recap: it did something now.

yay bare minimum function#

In its extremely primitive state (both the game and the entire networking code on both sides) I had actually managed to cobble together something that COULD function in the future! Here’s a non-exhaustive list of what works:

  1. Players can gasp JOIN THE GAME!!!
  2. Players can see eachother when they connect, along with a name!
  3. Players can see eachother move around!

Here’s some fun broken stuff:

  1. Changing rooms unloads everyone for the person leaving, and they will never see those people again
  2. Other players can still see people that are in other rooms moving around nonsensically
  3. Player positions are set to 0,0 when you join so unless they move they’re not where you see them
